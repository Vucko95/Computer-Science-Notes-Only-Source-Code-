    <hgroup>
        <h1>Young Scientists lauded for ethical focus on climate change</h1>
                            <h2> Contributions critical to advancing Paris Agreement, Higgins tells EU contest in RDS
            </h2>
            </hgroup>

        <div class="article_bodycopy">
                                        <p class="no_name">President <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_person=Michael+D+Higgins">Michael D Higgins</a> has saluted young scientists for their independence of thought, critical turn of mind and questioning of the status quo.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">Speaking at the opening of the European Union Contest for Young Scientists (EUCYS) at the RDS in Dublin on Saturday, Mr Higgins said such qualities were often combined with an ethical concern for the community and the planet. They were essential to meeting the challenges facing the world today, especially from climate change.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">Some of you have sought to push barriers in the field of mathematics; to investigate causes of and solutions to a variety of social problems, or to pose and answer questions of startling originality, he added. A total of 134 students from 38 countries are competing for the EU Young Scientist of the Year 2018 title.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">The President said he was particularly pleased some contestants were focused on the great challenge of climate change and its threat to our environment and natural habitats.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">The contributions of young scientists were critical to putting actions on the words of the 2015 Paris Agreement on climate change, he believed. Within <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_location=Europe">Europe</a>, the absence of borders in exchanging ideas, science and technology was essential, he said, as it would enable quicker identification of solutions in the best interests of the planet.</p>
                                                        <figure class="inline__content inline__content--image"> 
 <img alt="Audience members applaud at the opening ceremony for the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times" height="349" src="/polopoly_fs/1.3630767!/image/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/landscape_620/image.jpg" width="620"> 
 <figcaption>
   Audience members applaud at the opening ceremony for the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times 
 </figcaption> 
</figure><p class="no_name">More than any other place in the world, the continent of <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_location=Africa">Africa</a> is now the crucible for the global challenges that we confront, Mr Higgins said. Africa could in future decades be seen as a great opportunity, or a problem. Its only problem is we neglect issues of climate change and sustainability, he said.</p>
                                                                                                                                                                                            <p class="no_name">An equitable sharing of global technological advances and their application in raising the welfare of communities across the globe was also necessary, Mr Higgins said.</p>
                        <div class="adwrapper">
                <div id='div-gpt-ad-InContent-1' class="ad-inline-article">

                                                        <h4 class="crosshead">Pinnacle of scientific achievement</h4><p class="no_name">The President paid tribute to the competition which is at the pinnacle of scientific achievement and discovery amongst young people worldwide. It was also one of the great success stories of Europe.</p>
                                                        <figure class="inline__content inline__content--image"> 
 <img alt="Eva Khukhashvili from Georgia at her stand, Violence and Possible ways of its Prevention, at the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times" height="349" src="/polopoly_fs/1.3630766!/image/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/landscape_620/image.jpg" width="620"> 
 <figcaption>
   Eva Khukhashvili from Georgia at her stand, Violence and Possible ways of its Prevention, at the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times 
 </figcaption> 
</figure><p class="no_name">If in the past science was incorporated into a push to consume, I think science is even more important now as it enables us to recover...in the service of sustainability, Mr Higgins said.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">He noted a new young philosopher of the year award had been introduced in <a href="/news">Ireland</a> recently. He looked forward in years ahead to what would emerge from a meeting of the young philosophers of the year with young scientists of the year.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">The President met contestants including <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_person=Simon+Meehan">Simon Meehan</a> from <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_company=Ballincollig">Ballincollig</a>, Co Cork, current BT Young Scientist &amp; Technologist of the Year awardholder, who is representing Ireland, and <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_person=Josh+Mitchell">Josh Mitchell</a> (18), from <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_person=Holmes+Chapel">Holmes Chapel</a> near Manchester, who has developed a cheap, easy-to-build 3D printer, which he hopes will become widely available to ordinary consumers and come flat-packed in a container the size of a pizza box. He has developed eight prototypes since the age of 13.</p>
                                                        <figure class="inline__content inline__content--image"> 
 <img alt="Josh Luke Mitchell with his low-cost 3D printer at the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times" height="349" src="/polopoly_fs/1.3630764!/image/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/landscape_620/image.jpg" width="620"> 
 <figcaption>
   Josh Luke Mitchell with his low-cost 3D printer at the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times 
 </figcaption> 
</figure><p class="no_name">An initiative of the <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_organisation=European+Commission">European Commission</a> under the Science and Society programme, EUCYS is the annual showcase of the best of European student scientific achievement, but it is also open to other countries. A total of 88 projects is on display in the RDS, covering a wide range of topics  from the representation of gangsterism and scientific progress in comic books (Luxembourg) to a smart weather system simulator (Tunisia).</p>

                                                        <p class="no_name">Categories range across biology, physics, chemistry, computing, social sciences, environment, mathematics, materials, engineering and medicine. A judging panel headed by Prof <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_person=Tony+Fagan">Tony Fagan</a> of UCD interviews contestants and evaluates projects in a five-stage process.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">First place winners of EUCYS will be awarded 7,000 as well as a trip to the <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_organisation=Cern">Cern</a> research facility in <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_location=Switzerland">Switzerland</a> and the <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_organisation=European+Space+Agency">European Space Agency</a>. The EU Young Scientist for 2018 will be announced on Tuesday at an awards ceremony in the Printworks, Dublin Castle.</p>

 <img alt="Daniel Zion Kang of the USA with his paintable electronics at the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times" height="349" src="/polopoly_fs/1.3630763!/image/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/landscape_620/image.jpg" width="620"> 
 <figcaption>
   Daniel Zion Kang of the USA with his paintable electronics at the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times 
 </figcaption> 
</figure><p class="no_name">To mark the 30th EUCYS and the history of the BT Young Scientist &amp; Technology Exhibition, many of Irelands past winners, including the very first winner of BTYSTE in 1965, <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_person=John+Monahan">John Monahan</a>, returned to the RDS on Saturday to support Irelands current entry and to celebrate their collective journeys since their participation.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">We also want to take this moment to acknowledge Irelands past achievements at EUCYS and welcome back former participants who competed for their country down the years. This is a special moment for them and for all involved in EUCYS and the national qualifying competition, the BTYSTE, said <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_person=Mari+Cahalane">Mari Cahalane</a>, head of EUCYS 2018.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">The contest is open to the public in the RDS daily from 10am to 4pm, from Saturday to Monday next. More information on tickets and science shows being staged at the RDS to coincide with EUCYS 2018 is available at <a href="https://eucys2018.com  ">https://eucys2018.com</a></p>

                                        <p class="no_name">President <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_person=Michael+D+Higgins">Michael D Higgins</a> has saluted young scientists for their independence of thought, critical turn of mind and questioning of the status quo.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">Speaking at the opening of the European Union Contest for Young Scientists (EUCYS) at the RDS in Dublin on Saturday, Mr Higgins said such qualities were often combined with an ethical concern for the community and the planet. They were essential to meeting the challenges facing the world today, especially from climate change.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">Some of you have sought to push barriers in the field of mathematics; to investigate causes of and solutions to a variety of social problems, or to pose and answer questions of startling originality, he added. A total of 134 students from 38 countries are competing for the EU Young Scientist of the Year 2018 title.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">The President said he was particularly pleased some contestants were focused on the great challenge of climate change and its threat to our environment and natural habitats.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">The contributions of young scientists were critical to putting actions on the words of the 2015 Paris Agreement on climate change, he believed. Within <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_location=Europe">Europe</a>, the absence of borders in exchanging ideas, science and technology was essential, he said, as it would enable quicker identification of solutions in the best interests of the planet.</p>
                                                        <figure class="inline__content inline__content--image"> 
 <img alt="Audience members applaud at the opening ceremony for the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times" height="349" src="/polopoly_fs/1.3630767!/image/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/landscape_620/image.jpg" width="620"> 
 <figcaption>
   Audience members applaud at the opening ceremony for the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times 
 </figcaption> 
</figure><p class="no_name">More than any other place in the world, the continent of <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_location=Africa">Africa</a> is now the crucible for the global challenges that we confront, Mr Higgins said. Africa could in future decades be seen as a great opportunity, or a problem. Its only problem is we neglect issues of climate change and sustainability, he said.</p>
                                                                                                                                                                                            <p class="no_name">An equitable sharing of global technological advances and their application in raising the welfare of communities across the globe was also necessary, Mr Higgins said.</p>

                                                        <h4 class="crosshead">Pinnacle of scientific achievement</h4><p class="no_name">The President paid tribute to the competition which is at the pinnacle of scientific achievement and discovery amongst young people worldwide. It was also one of the great success stories of Europe.</p>
                                                        <figure class="inline__content inline__content--image"> 
 <img alt="Eva Khukhashvili from Georgia at her stand, Violence and Possible ways of its Prevention, at the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times" height="349" src="/polopoly_fs/1.3630766!/image/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/landscape_620/image.jpg" width="620"> 
 <figcaption>
   Eva Khukhashvili from Georgia at her stand, Violence and Possible ways of its Prevention, at the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times 
 </figcaption> 
</figure><p class="no_name">If in the past science was incorporated into a push to consume, I think science is even more important now as it enables us to recover...in the service of sustainability, Mr Higgins said.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">He noted a new young philosopher of the year award had been introduced in <a href="/news">Ireland</a> recently. He looked forward in years ahead to what would emerge from a meeting of the young philosophers of the year with young scientists of the year.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">The President met contestants including <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_person=Simon+Meehan">Simon Meehan</a> from <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_company=Ballincollig">Ballincollig</a>, Co Cork, current BT Young Scientist &amp; Technologist of the Year awardholder, who is representing Ireland, and <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_person=Josh+Mitchell">Josh Mitchell</a> (18), from <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_person=Holmes+Chapel">Holmes Chapel</a> near Manchester, who has developed a cheap, easy-to-build 3D printer, which he hopes will become widely available to ordinary consumers and come flat-packed in a container the size of a pizza box. He has developed eight prototypes since the age of 13.</p>
                                                        <figure class="inline__content inline__content--image"> 
 <img alt="Josh Luke Mitchell with his low-cost 3D printer at the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times" height="349" src="/polopoly_fs/1.3630764!/image/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/landscape_620/image.jpg" width="620"> 
 <figcaption>
   Josh Luke Mitchell with his low-cost 3D printer at the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times 
 </figcaption> 
</figure><p class="no_name">An initiative of the <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_organisation=European+Commission">European Commission</a> under the Science and Society programme, EUCYS is the annual showcase of the best of European student scientific achievement, but it is also open to other countries. A total of 88 projects is on display in the RDS, covering a wide range of topics  from the representation of gangsterism and scientific progress in comic books (Luxembourg) to a smart weather system simulator (Tunisia).</p>

                                                        <p class="no_name">Categories range across biology, physics, chemistry, computing, social sciences, environment, mathematics, materials, engineering and medicine. A judging panel headed by Prof <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_person=Tony+Fagan">Tony Fagan</a> of UCD interviews contestants and evaluates projects in a five-stage process.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">First place winners of EUCYS will be awarded 7,000 as well as a trip to the <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_organisation=Cern">Cern</a> research facility in <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_location=Switzerland">Switzerland</a> and the <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_organisation=European+Space+Agency">European Space Agency</a>. The EU Young Scientist for 2018 will be announced on Tuesday at an awards ceremony in the Printworks, Dublin Castle.</p>
                                                        <figure class="inline__content inline__content--image"> 
 <img alt="Daniel Zion Kang of the USA with his paintable electronics at the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times" height="349" src="/polopoly_fs/1.3630763!/image/image.jpg_gen/derivatives/landscape_620/image.jpg" width="620"> 
 <figcaption>
   Daniel Zion Kang of the USA with his paintable electronics at the European Union Contest for Young Scientists at the RDS. Photograph: Cyril Byrne/The Irish Times 
 </figcaption> 
</figure><p class="no_name">To mark the 30th EUCYS and the history of the BT Young Scientist &amp; Technology Exhibition, many of Irelands past winners, including the very first winner of BTYSTE in 1965, <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_person=John+Monahan">John Monahan</a>, returned to the RDS on Saturday to support Irelands current entry and to celebrate their collective journeys since their participation.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">We also want to take this moment to acknowledge Irelands past achievements at EUCYS and welcome back former participants who competed for their country down the years. This is a special moment for them and for all involved in EUCYS and the national qualifying competition, the BTYSTE, said <a class="search" href="/topics/topics-7.1213540?article=true&amp;tag_person=Mari+Cahalane">Mari Cahalane</a>, head of EUCYS 2018.</p>
                                                        <p class="no_name">The contest is open to the public in the RDS daily from 10am to 4pm, from Saturday to Monday next. More information on tickets and science shows being staged at the RDS to coincide with EUCYS 2018 is available at <a href="https://eucys2018.com  ">https://eucys2018.com</a></p>
